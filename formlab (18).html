New here? How to use FormLab — tap to open

FormLab turns phone video into coaching feedback: measure angles and timing, grade form with a green/yellow/red key, track shooting, and get drills aimed at what needs work. You can explore everything below without setting anything up — you don't need an athlete or any data to browse workouts and drills.

The tabs, in order:

Athletes — add anyone you coach (name + sport is enough; add bodyweight if you want force estimates). Everything else attaches to the athlete you pick. Start here when you're ready to save and track.

Analyze — the heart of it. Pick an athlete, choose or record a video, and it appears with a scrubber and frame-step buttons. Then you can:
• Angle tool — tap 3 points (middle tap is the joint) to measure any joint angle.
• Form key — pick a checkpoint for the athlete's sport and the lines turn green (good), yellow (tighten up), or red (focus) against target ranges.
• Detect skeleton / Auto-track — the AI finds the body and reads angles for you.
• Mark button (under the video) — step to the exact foot strike or takeoff and tap it for contact time, jump height, and force estimates.
• 🏀 Shot tracking — start a session, tap the court where they shoot, hit MAKE/MISS as shots land; use the ◠ Arc tool from the side to grade release angle.
• ⛳ Golf swing — pick the club, follow the 4 taps to measure club head speed, and use the Coil buttons for X-factor. Posture, lead arm, trail elbow, and impact grade through the Form key like any sport.

Log entry — type in anything you measured by hand (jump height, sprint time, game stats). Combines with video numbers in Progress.

Workouts — browse ready-made sessions by sport and level (no athlete needed), edit them, stack two into a hybrid, and save to your library. Any yellow/red form grade shows up here as targeted drills. Build a weekly plan and pick a test day once an athlete is added.

Calendar — see planned workouts, logged sessions, measurements, and shooting % day by day.

Progress — chart any metric over time and generate a report.

Learn — plain-English glossary of basketball and track terms with the cue and how to check each on film.

A good first 10 minutes:

1. Add yourself or one athlete on this tab.
2. Go to Workouts, scroll to Build a workout, pick a sport + level, and hit Load template to see how sessions look.
3. Go to Analyze, load a short clip, and try the Angle tool + pick a Form-key checkpoint to watch the colors.
4. For hoops: start a Shot session and log a few MAKE/MISS taps.
5. Check Progress and Calendar to see it all land.

📹 Filming tip: film the same view and distance every time (side for jumps/sprints/shots, front for slides/cuts), keep the whole body in frame, and use slo-mo (240fps) for anything fast — the numbers are only comparable when the clips match. Timing & force results

Mark frames with the Mark button right under the video — step frame-by-frame to the exact foot strike or takeoff, then tap it. Results appear here.

Force and jump height are physics-based estimates from your frame timing — not force-plate measurements. Accuracy depends on marking the exact contact frames, so step frame-by-frame in slo-mo.

Shot tracking basketball

Side view: grade the arc — tap Auto-arc, then tap the ball once at release to trace the whole flight with a dotted line (or use the ◠ Arc tool manually). Front view: track makes — start a session, tap the court where they're shooting from, then hit MAKE / MISS under the video as each shot lands.

Golf swing speed & coil

Film face-on (chest facing camera) for coil and club speed, down-the-line (behind, looking at the target) for posture and plane. Use 240fps slo-mo — the club moves too far between frames at 30fps. Grade posture, lead arm, trail elbow, and impact with the Form key above like any other sport.

Coil / X-factor — the torque proxy

How much the shoulders out-turn the hips at the top — the coil that stores power. Face-on view: detect the skeleton at address, mark it, then step to the top of the backswing, detect again, mark it. Estimated from how much each line foreshortens; ~35–55° is the classic good zone. A 2D estimate, not a lab measurement.

Form guide — what “good” looks like

Target ranges from coaching and biomechanics literature. They're starting points — body types and styles vary, so treat big deviations as questions to investigate, not automatic faults. Measure with the Angle tool or Auto-track on the matching frame.

Basketball — jump shot
• Set point: elbow bent ~75–90° with the upper arm near parallel to the floor — measure elbow angle on the frame where the ball sits in the shot pocket.
• Dip/load: deeper knee and hip bend for longer range — proficient shooters sink lower before rising. Compare knee angle at deepest dip across shot distances.
• Release: ball leaves near the top of the jump, arm finishing at roughly 45–60° toward the rim; research puts good release trajectories around ~49–55° above horizontal for most mid-range shots (lower from deep).
• Follow-through: elbow reaches full extension (~180°) — freeze the frame just after release and check.
• Balance: jump and land near the same spot — use the Line tool as a vertical reference from the takeoff point.
• Defensive stance: feet shoulder-width or a bit wider, knees bent to roughly 100–130°, butt down and back (not leaning forward), chest up, weight on the balls of the feet, hands active around waist height. Freeze a stance frame and measure the knee angle.
• Defensive slide: short, choppy steps — push off the trail leg, the lead leg reaches out, feet stay close to (not fully crossing) each other. The stance should hold the whole time — standing up between slides is the most common breakdown coaches flag. Film 10s from the front, run Auto-track, and read the clip summary: average knee angle is stance depth, and it counts every time the athlete rises out of it (>150° knee angle).
• Push-off force (est.): auto-track also estimates the horizontal force of each slide push-off from hip movement and the athlete's bodyweight — the same physics-estimate approach as the jump timing tool. Useful for tracking whether push-off power is improving, not a lab measurement.
• Standard drills (from coaching literature): Lane slides — slide baseline to baseline across the key without crossing feet; Zig-zag / mirror drill — partner-led change-of-direction slides; Closeout — sprint out under control, chop feet, hands up, settle into stance; Stance holds — hold the position for time to build strength and habit.
Track — sprint & explosiveness
• Acceleration (first 10m): whole-body lean ~45° with shins angled forward at push-off — measure shin-to-ground with the Angle tool.
• Max velocity posture: tall and upright, thigh rising to near parallel with the ground (front-side mechanics) — measure hip angle at peak knee drive.
• Ground contact: foot strikes under the hips, not out in front — Line tool vertical from the hip; use Timing & force sprint-step mode for contact time.
• Arms: elbows near ~90°, driving from the shoulder.
• Vertical jump: countermovement to roughly 80–110° of knee bend, then full hip-knee-ankle extension at takeoff — pair the knee angle with Timing & force jump mode.
Soccer — instep kick
• Plant foot: lands beside the ball, pointing at the target — Line tool from plant foot through the ball.
• Backswing: kicking knee flexes deeply (past ~90°) before whipping to near-full extension at contact — measure knee angle at deepest cock and again at ball contact.
• Hip drive: the thigh decelerates as the shin whips through — the knee-angle change between those two frames is the power signature.
• Trunk: slight lean over the ball keeps shots down.
Golf — full swing
• Address posture: athletic hinge from the hips — hip angle (shoulder–hip–knee) roughly 125–155°, knees softly flexed (~140–165°), arms hanging under the shoulders. Dead-straight legs or a squat both grade out.
• Top of backswing: lead arm stays close to straight (elbow ~160–180°) for width; trail elbow folds to roughly 90° (“waiter’s tray”).
• Coil / X-factor: shoulders turn well past the hips — the differential (~35–55°) is the stored torque. Use the Coil buttons in the ⛳ Golf card, face-on view.
• Impact: weight into the lead side, lead knee posting toward straight (~140–172°), hips already open while the chest catches up.
• Club head speed: the club is a known length, so two taps near impact plus the frame timing give mph — same physics-estimate approach as the jump force tool. References (approx., TrackMan-style norms): driver — avg male amateur ~93 mph, low-handicap ~105, Tour ~115; 7-iron — amateur ~75, Tour ~90.
• Tempo: classic backswing-to-downswing ratio is about 3:1 — count frames from takeaway to top vs top to impact.
Football — stance & first step
• Stance: hips loaded with knees around 90–110°, back flat — measure knee and hip angle in the frozen stance frame.
• First step: positive shin angle (~45°) driving forward, pads staying low — same acceleration mechanics as the sprint start.
• Change of direction: on cuts, watch the knee tracking over the foot rather than caving inward — the AI skeleton makes a knock-kneed collapse easy to spot from a front-on clip.
• Contact time: use Timing & force sprint-step mode on the plant step of a cut — quicker off the ground with the same angle is progress.

Learn

The vocabulary behind the video work — pick a sport, then tap any term for what it means, the coaching cue, and how to check it on film.

Basketball

Set point
Where the ball sits just before the upward shooting motion — usually around forehead height with the elbow bent ~75–90° and under the ball. Cue: "ball in the pocket, elbow under it." On film: freeze the pocket frame, grade the elbow with the Form key.
Follow-through
Full arm extension after release, wrist snapped, fingers pointing at the rim ("hand in the cookie jar"). Cue: "hold the finish until the ball hits." On film: the frame just after release — elbow should grade green near full extension.
Shot arc
The trajectory of the ball. Research on good shooters puts the release angle around ~48–55° for most shots — flat shots leave almost no margin at the rim, moon balls are hard to repeat. Cue: "shoot up, not at." On film: side view, ◠ Arc tool — tap release, tap the peak.
Dip / load
The small downward knee/hip bend before rising into the shot — it syncs rhythm and adds power for range. Skilled shooters usually dip deeper from distance. On film: knee angle at the deepest point before the rise.
Triple threat
Catch position where the player can still shoot, pass, or drive — knees bent, ball protected on the hip, eyes up. Cue: "catch low, stay dangerous."
Closeout
Sprinting at a shooter under control: long strides first, then short chopping steps, one hand up, settling into stance without flying past. On film: front view — do the feet chop and does the stance knee grade green on arrival?
Defensive stance & slide
Butt down and back, chest up, knees ~100–130°, weight on the balls of the feet. Sliding: push off the trail leg, feet never fully cross, stance never stands up. On film: Auto-track a 10s slide clip — the summary counts every stance break.
Box out
On the shot: find your player, make contact, reverse pivot into them with a wide low base, hold, then go get the ball. Position first, rebound second.
Pick and roll
A teammate sets a screen on the ball-handler's defender, then rolls to the basket. The two-player game most offenses are built on. Defensive answers you'll hear: drop (big sags back), hedge (big jumps out then recovers), ICE (force the ball away from the screen, toward the sideline).
Euro step & jump stop
Euro step: two steps in different directions to slide past a defender at the rim. Jump stop: landing on both feet at once — either foot can then pivot. On film: plant-step contact time in sprint-step mode; quicker off the floor with control is progress.

Golf

Address / posture
The setup: hinged from the hips (not slumped from the spine), knees softly flexed, arms hanging under the shoulders, weight balanced. Most swing faults trace back here. On film: down-the-line frame — grade hip hinge and knee flex with the Form key.
X-factor (coil)
The gap between shoulder turn and hip turn at the top — shoulders ~90°, hips ~45°, differential ~35–55°. That stretch across the torso is the stored torque the downswing releases. On film: face-on — Coil buttons in the ⛳ Golf card.
Lag & casting
Lag is keeping the wrist angle between lead arm and shaft deep into the downswing — releasing it early (“casting”) leaks speed before the ball. On film: down-the-line at 240fps — angle tool on lead arm → wrist → club head at delivery.
Swing plane
The tilted circle the club travels on. From down-the-line, draw a Line-tool reference through the shaft at address — a huge loop above or under it on the way down is worth investigating.
Tempo (3:1)
Good players’ backswings take about three times as long as the downswing, whatever their overall speed. On film: count frames takeaway→top, then top→impact — the ratio matters more than the raw count.
Weight shift & post
Pressure moves into the trail side going back, then aggressively into the lead side coming down — finishing with the lead leg “posted” (straightening) at impact while the hips open. Hanging back on the trail foot costs strike and speed. On film: impact frame — lead-knee-post checkpoint.
Club head speed
Speed of the head through impact — the biggest single driver of distance (roughly 2.5–2.7 yards of carry per mph with the driver). Averages: male amateur driver ~93 mph, Tour ~115. On film: ⚡ button in the ⛳ Golf card — 240fps, face-on.
Early extension
The hips thrusting toward the ball in the downswing, standing the posture up — the most common amateur fault on camera. On film: down-the-line — Line tool vertically on the backside at address; watch whether the hips leave it before impact.

Track & Field — sprints

Block start / set position
Front knee roughly ~90°, rear knee ~110–130° in the set position, hips slightly above shoulders, eyes down. The gun should fire the athlete out low, not up. On film: freeze the set frame and measure both knees; then check shin angles on steps 1–3.
Drive phase (0–20m)
The pushing part of the race: whole-body lean around ~45°, shins angled forward, big pushes behind the body, head neutral. Standing up early kills acceleration. On film: shin-to-ground angle at push-off — grade it with the Form key.
Transition
Roughly 20–40m: the gradual rise from the drive lean to upright max-velocity posture. It should look smooth over many steps, never a sudden pop-up.
Max velocity & front-side mechanics
Tall posture, thigh rising near parallel in front (front-side), foot striking under the hips — most of the leg action happening in front of the body, not trailing behind. On film: hip angle at peak knee drive, plus a vertical Line-tool reference from the hip at touchdown.
Ground contact time
How long the foot is on the track each step — elite sprinters at top speed are under ~0.10s. Shorter contacts at the same stride length mean more stiffness and speed. On film: 240fps + Timing & force sprint-step mode.
Stride length vs stride frequency
Speed = length × frequency. Chasing longer strides by reaching in front (overstriding) is braking; length should come from force into the ground, not reach. Count steps over a known distance on video to track both.
Arm action
Elbows near ~90°, driving from the shoulder, hands moving roughly hip-to-cheek, no crossing the midline. Arms set the rhythm the legs follow. On film: elbow carriage checkpoint in the Form key.
A-skip / B-skip / wickets
The classic drill family: A-skips groove the knee lift and downward strike, B-skips add the leg extension-and-pull, wicket runs (mini-hurdles at set spacing) force upright posture and front-side shape at speed.
Speed endurance vs tempo
Speed endurance: near-max runs (e.g. 120–150m) with full recovery — holding top speed longer. Tempo: relaxed ~65–75% runs with short rests — building the engine and rehearsing mechanics without strain. Different days, different purposes; don't blend them.
RSI (reactive strength index)
Jump height divided by ground contact time on a rebound jump — a springiness score. On film: the Timing & force rebound work in the plyo templates feeds this; watch the trend in Progress.

Football

Three-point stance
One hand down, feet staggered, knees ~90–110°, back flat, weight coiled forward — loaded to fire, not resting. On film: freeze the pre-snap frame — stance knee checkpoint in the Form key.
Get-off / first step
The race is won in the first step: shin angled ~45° forward, pushing the ground back, body staying low. Popping straight up kills the rep. On film: 240fps side view — first-step shin checkpoint.
Pad level
"Low man wins." Playing beneath the opponent’s pads with bent knees and a flat back — leverage comes from the legs, never from bending at the waist.
Backpedal & hip flip
Defensive backs: smooth pedal with weight over the toes, then one clean hip turn to run — no false steps. On film: count frames from the break cue to full sprint.
Tackling form
Eyes up, head across the body, wrap and drive through with the legs — never lead with the crown of the helmet. Form protects the tackler as much as it finishes the play.
Route stem & break
Receivers: sell the route at full speed (the stem), then sink the hips and cut (the break) without rounding it off. On film: plant-knee bend on the break — cut mechanics.
Landing mechanics
Every jump ball and every cut ends in a landing: knees bent and tracking over the toes, quiet feet, chest up. Stiff straight-leg landings are where knees get hurt. On film: the landing checkpoint grades it.

Soccer

Instep drive
The power strike: laces through the middle of the ball, ankle locked, knee whipping from deep bend to full extension at contact. On film: backswing and contact checkpoints in the Form key.
Plant foot
The non-kicking foot sets everything: planted beside the ball, pointing at the target, knee softly bent. A bad plant makes a good strike impossible.
Backswing / leg whip
Power loads in the knee cock — the deeper the backswing, the more whip through the ball. On film: deepest knee angle before the swing forward.
Follow-through
The kicking leg keeps traveling toward the target after contact, body over the ball for a driven shot, leaning back for height. Where the follow-through points, the ball usually goes.
First touch
Cushioning the ball into space with the foot, thigh, or chest — the touch that sets up the next action instead of stopping play. Judge it by where the ball ends up, not how it looks.
Cutting & change of direction
Plant wide, sink the hips, push off the outside leg with the knee tracking over the toes — never caving inward. On film: front view, cut-plant checkpoint.
Shielding
Body between defender and ball, arm feeling contact, knees bent, ball on the far foot. Strength position, not a foul position.
